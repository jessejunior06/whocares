Who Cares
A meditation on caring, indifference, and self-preservation
Gothic Indifference
The Genesis
"Who cares?" — this statement has been circulating in my mind for some time. It emerged from sitting down and genuinely thinking about life experiences and the reality of how we exist as humans.
The question isn't really a question at all. It's an observation, a conclusion reached after careful examination: How many people really care?
The Universal Question
We live in a world where everyone claims to care, yet when push comes to shove, genuine care is scarce. Social media likes, surface-level check-ins, performative empathy — but real, deep, stick-around-when-it-gets-messy care? That's rare.
The question becomes less about whether people should care and more about whether caring itself is worth the energy when it's so rarely reciprocated.
The Hard Truths
  • No one really cares about the pain others go through because everyone's got their own problems. We're all drowning in our individual storms, too busy surviving to notice someone else's struggle.
  • People often don't value what they have until it's gone. Effort goes unnoticed, investment unreturned, and eventually you realize that your energy might be better spent elsewhere.
  • Sometimes indifference becomes self-preservation. When caring consistently leads to disappointment, the natural response is to stop caring. It's not cruelty — it's evolution.
The Design Philosophy
The Gothic blackletter isn't decorative — it's architectural. These words deserve weight, permanence, the gravity of stone tablets. The dripping effects aren't stylistic choices; they're visual tears, the slow bleed of caring too much until you're empty.
The commercial elements below — QR code, brand logo, barcode — transform this profound human experience into a product. Because that's what we do now: we commodify our pain, package our revelations, make our deepest truths consumable.
"Who cares?" isn't cynicism. It's clarity. It's the answer to every one-sided effort, every unreciprocated gesture, every moment of wondering if your investment matters. Sometimes the most liberating realization is that the answer might just be: no one. And maybe that's perfectly fine.
